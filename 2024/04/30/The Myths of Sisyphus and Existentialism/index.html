Why "The Myth of Sisyphus" Is Difficult to Understand

2024-04-30

Albert Camus’s “The Myth of Sisyphus” is, strictly speaking, a collection of Camus’s philosophical essays. The well-known story about Sisyphus pushing the rock only to have it fall down is actually just in the last small section of the collection and is very brief. Most of the book explains Camus’s philosophy of the absurd, which is more important than the section “The Myth of Sisyphus.”

The Relationship Between Camus and Existentialism
It’s problematic to laud Camus as a representative figure of “existentialism.” Aside from Camus’s novels like “The Stranger” and “The Plague,” looking solely at “The Myth of Sisyphus,” Camus’s own thought is quite unique. Although I haven’t yet read “The Stranger,” the novels indeed contain elements the author cannot explain, and it may be correct to consider Camus’s novels representative of existentialist literature. However, from most of “The Myth of Sisyphus” — “The Absurd Reasoning,” “The Absurd Man,” “The Absurd Creation” — where Camus’s own thoughts are discussed, Camus’s “philosophy of the absurd” is more closely connected to life itself compared to existentialism represented truly by Heidegger and Sartre. Camus starts with the notion of “suicide,” but the meanings of “not committing suicide” and “living” are closer. In contrast, the “existence” discussed by Sartre and others is more metaphysical, more “conceptual,” or rather the semantics of the word “existence” itself — so true existentialism inherited its method from Husserl’s phenomenology. But Camus’s “philosophy of the absurd” is clearly more “down-to-earth,” drawing much from life’s experiences. This includes in “The Myth of Sisyphus,” where Camus criticizes Husserl’s phenomenology in the section on “philosophical suicide” (which also critiques Christian existentialism) and advocates narrowing the scope of freedom in the section “The Freedom of the Absurd.” Thus, Camus’s thoughts are not very “existentialist.” Indeed, he himself did not claim to be an existentialist.

Moreover, Sartre believed that existence precedes essence, but while reading “The Myth of Sisyphus,” I did not feel that Camus’s ideas suggested existence precedes essence.

However, this is not a complete denial of the relationship between Camus’s philosophy of the absurd and existentialism; their connection is deeper than my current understanding. For example, both Camus’s “philosophy of the absurd” and Sartre’s “existentialism” emphasize human transcendence — this is the most superficial layer. Thus, the conclusion is that Camus’s philosophy of the absurd is closely related to existentialism, but equating Camus’s philosophy with existentialism is inappropriate.

Why “The Myth of Sisyphus” Is Difficult to Understand
“The Myth of Sisyphus” is a difficult book to read, but it is not a philosophical work per se; it should only be considered a part of Camus’s “philosophy of the absurd.” Camus passed away in a car accident before he could write his philosophical works. My own reading this time was increasingly quick, and I glossed over many parts without delving deeply. Why it’s difficult to understand and how to comprehend this book, I have my answers.

The first part concerns the work itself.

Firstly, this is not a philosophical work skilled in logic. It is not a rigorously argumentative piece like Sartre’s “Being and Nothingness.” It is Camus’s prose, lacking in the process of argumentation. The author’s thinking is jumpy, which adds another layer of reading pressure for the reader, who must guess how Camus’s logic jumps in order to “understand.”

Second, the book is very literary, with many philosophical ideas expressed through poetic language. This strong literary nature, an extreme case being Nietzsche’s “Thus Spoke Zarathustra,” although Camus still speaks “human language,” for the most part, one can understand what Camus wants to say. This point, combined with the first, contributes to the textual reading difficulty.

Third is the issue of translation. Fortunately, there are various translations available on the market now. As someone who doesn’t understand French, I can later compare different translations to get a side idea of Camus’s original intentions.

The second part concerns the reader’s reasons. Overall, it’s a lack of experience.

Firstly, the most important is that readers lacking life experience will find it very difficult to understand Camus’s theories. I can assert that reading this book at different stages of life—20, 30, 40 years old—the feelings will definitely be different. So far, I personally can only understand in the section “The Absurd Wall,” that absurdity arises from “repetition,” “time,” and “death.” However, I don’t have much personal life experience with absurdity arising from “the unfamiliar.” Additionally, the section “The Freedom of the Absurd” was very confusing to me. But this is normal; Camus’s “philosophy of the absurd” stems more from life experiences than from philosophical concepts (Camus also examined the manifestations of the absurd, rather than starting from the concept of the absurd itself), so understanding doesn’t solely depend on knowing philosophical concepts; it requires support from life experiences.

Secondly, a lack of reading experience is another factor. To understand Camus’s “The Myth of Sisyphus,” philosophically, one needs a deep understanding of Christianity, mysticism, existentialism, phenomenology, and more. Literarily, one should have read works by Dostoevsky and Kafka, as Camus uses characters from these authors extensively to explain the concept of “The Absurd Man.” Additionally, a certain background in drama (especially tragedy), including ancient Greek tragedy and Shakespeare, is necessary.

So, the reason I read quickly is because I knew I lacked the preparation necessary to understand the book beforehand, and it was not possible to understand it fully without this. Preparation is a very lengthy process that not only requires a long time to experience life but also to enrich one’s knowledge base.

Why We Should Read ‘The Myth of Sisyphus’
It’s normal not to understand, but that doesn’t mean we shouldn’t read it now because, in “The Myth of Sisyphus,” many conclusions of the “philosophy of the absurd” are evident, such as the only philosophical attitude harmonious with the absurd is defiance.

We can know the conclusions, but the proofs of these conclusions need to be experienced in life. Each age will definitely feel differently, and in the later insights of life, readers can also gain “epiphanic” experiences, deepening their understanding of Camus’s “philosophy of the absurd.” Some of the finer conclusions Camus writes are quite subtle, needing time to refine before we can understand what Camus said. In short, theory and practice are intertwined.

Reading “The Myth of Sisyphus” is like implanting an idea in our subconscious that maybe at the right moment, we can suddenly understand the depth of Camus’s thoughts, or think of what Camus said and look back at it, which is the meaning of reading.

There is no need to feel ashamed or sad about not understanding; this kind of temporary “putting aside” style of reading is actually the best way to understand many classic works. It’s okay not to understand now; just revisit it every few years.

“The Myth of Sisyphus” was published in 1942 when Camus was about thirty years old. Considering that I am not a philosophy major, and given the differences between the current social environment and the World War II environment Camus lived in, I hope that between the ages of thirty and forty, I will basically understand what Camus was saying, since I now understand about a third of it.
